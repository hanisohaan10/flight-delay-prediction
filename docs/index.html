How it works

No crystal ball — just disciplined data

The hardest part of predicting delays isn't the model. It's using only what you'd actually know two hours before takeoff, and nothing that leaks from after the flight.

1

Schedule in

Airline, route, day and departure hour — all known the moment a ticket is sold.

2

History added

Each airport and airline carries its historical on-time record, learned from the training period only.

3

Model decides

A gradient-boosted tree ensemble (XGBoost) weighs 6 signals across 700 trees.

4

Probability out

You get a calibrated risk — and a breakdown of what pushed it up or down.

The model

Honest about what it can and can't do

Predicting delays from schedule data alone is genuinely hard. Here's the real performance — no inflated numbers.

0.65
ROC-AUC

Ranks a random delayed flight above an on-time one 65% of the time.

0.33
PR-AUC

Real lift over the 18% base rate at finding the minority — delayed — flights.

6
signals, 0 leaks

Only pre-departure features. Nothing from during or after the flight.

This is a screening signal, not a guarantee. Most flights leave on time, so treat the output as "how far above or below average is this flight's risk" — not a verdict. A high reading means watch the gate, not cancel your plans.

Predicts departure delay (>15 min), not arrival delay or cancellation. Built for education and demonstration; not affiliated with any airline or the BTS. No data leaves your device — the model runs entirely in your browser.

Sign-in is wired and ready

Tarmac uses Google Identity Services. To switch it on, drop your Google OAuth client ID into CONFIG.googleClientId in app.js, and add your site's address as an authorised origin in the Google Cloud console.

Everything here works without signing in — the predictor is open to anyone.
